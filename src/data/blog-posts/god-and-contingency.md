Classical theism posits a profound, asymmetrical relationship between God and creation. In this view, God is the singular, ultimate source of all existence, while creatures are entirely dependent on Him. The philosopher Charles Hartshorne, a key figure in process thought, summarized this traditional stance as, "One wholly uncreated, the other wholly uncreative."[^1]

This concept was systematically articulated by Thomas Aquinas, who built upon Aristotle's ideas of God as "pure act" (*actus purus*) and the "unmoved mover."[^2] For Aquinas, God's essence and existence are identical and entirely self-sufficient. This leads to the doctrine of divine impassibility: God is without feelings or emotions as we understand them, because emotions imply change. If God is "pure act," He is already everything He could possibly be.

Herein lies a paradox. Classical theism holds that God is all-knowing of a contingent world—a world where things could have been otherwise. Yet, it also holds that nothing in God's nature can be contingent. How can an utterly changeless being have knowledge of a changing world? If a contingent event were to happen differently, God's knowledge would have to be different to remain accurate. This suggests that the content of God's knowledge is, in fact, contingent.

### The Process Philosophy Solution

This is where the work of Alfred North Whitehead, and his student Hartshorne, offers a compelling alternative. Whitehead's process philosophy, most notably in his magnum opus *Process and Reality*, resolves this paradox by proposing a "dipolar" conception of God.[^3]

In this model, God has two "natures" or "poles":

1.  **The Primordial Nature:** This is God's abstract, eternal, and unchanging pole. It is the realm of pure potentiality, containing all possibilities for the universe. It is the "unmoved mover" in the sense that it is the ultimate source of novel forms and ideals, luring the world toward its creative becoming. This nature is "deficiently actual" because it is potential, not yet realized in the concrete world.

2.  **The Consequent Nature:** This is God's concrete, temporal, and ever-changing pole. It is the result of God's intimate relationship with the world. God "prehends" or takes in every event that occurs in creation. Every joy, sorrow, and decision is woven into the divine experience. Therefore, God's consequent nature is supremely contingent, constantly growing and evolving as the universe unfolds.

Whitehead famously described God as the "fellow-sufferer who understands."[^4] God is not a distant, impassible monarch, but an intimate participant in the cosmic process.

By distinguishing between God's eternal potentiality and God's temporal actuality, process theism allows for a deity who is both unchanging in essence (the primordial nature) and fully related to and affected by our contingent world (the consequent nature). The paradox dissolves, as God's knowledge of our world resides in the consequent nature, which is perfectly suited to encompass a dynamic and contingent reality.

[^1]: Charles Hartshorne, *Man's Vision of God and the Logic of Theism* (1941). Hartshorne critiques the classical model while developing his own dipolar view, heavily influenced by Whitehead.
[^2]: Thomas Aquinas, *Summa Theologica*, Part I, Question 2, Article 3, and Question 3, Article 1.
[^3]: Alfred North Whitehead, *Process and Reality: An Essay in Cosmology* (1929). The dipolar nature of God is a central theme of Part V.
[^4]: Alfred North Whitehead, *Process and Reality* (1929), Part V, Chapter II, Section V.
